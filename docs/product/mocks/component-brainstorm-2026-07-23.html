Four components, three directions each, rendered with the current light tokens (theme-direction pick from the pop brainstorm is independent — these compose with A/B/C). Problem: the flat primary-wash pills read as status labels, not options; the glyph micro-animations vanish against the lavender wash. All three directions put the glyph on a lighter dish so its stroke animation reads, and add an explicit-but-quiet "optional" hint so nobody feels obligated.

3 · "Finished something else? Log it too" — keep it, and here's why

Verdict first, styling second.

Should manual after-the-fact logging exist? Yes — emphatically.
  • Category standard (verified 2026-07): Toggl Track ships "Manual Mode" as a first-class peer of the timer — the "+" sits directly under the play button, framed as "for when you forget to turn on the timer". ATracker markets "log time after the fact" on its App Store listing; even auto-tracking Rize keeps a manual "+" on Home; Timery lets you tap any start time to edit. The only apps WITHOUT it are commitment timers (Forest, Opal, Session) whose mechanic is live presence — and Session users beg for retro-add in reviews. Whenbee is calibration-first = tracking-side of that split.
  • It feeds calibration: retro logs are not dead weight — they train the model at half weight (RETRO_ALPHA_FACTOR = 0.5 in engine/constants.ts, memory is noisier). More honest data, appropriately discounted. Removing the affordance would slow every user's ripening.
  • It rescues the miss case: the target user (time optimist) will regularly forget to start the timer. Retro is the no-guilt recovery path — killing it punishes exactly the person the app is for.
Placement: keep it on Today, but demote it from "grey card that looks like a dead setting" to a styled ghost-slot (below). On the reward screen and in 2B it can also live as a secondary door.
